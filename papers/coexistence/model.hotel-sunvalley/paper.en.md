### — A New Structure of Mutual Co-Creation Between Local Residents and Tourism Facilities —

## Abstract

The Nasu region has long developed as one of Japan’s major resort destinations. At the same time, it now faces structural challenges common to many regional communities in Japan, including population decline, an aging population, and the weakening of local community activities.

In response to these regional issues, this study focuses on large-scale accommodation facilities that function as receiving bases for tourists and support the wider tourism region. It examines the relationship among three actors: large-scale accommodation facilities, tourists, and local residents. Rather than understanding this relationship through conventional frameworks of consumption, labor, or competition, this study aims to reconstruct it as a “mutual co-creation structure” and presents both the practical research process and its observed outcomes.

Conventionally, tourism facilities and local residents have often been discussed in terms of competition, consumption, or service provision. In reality, however, while large-scale accommodation facilities support the tourism infrastructure of a region, local residents also enrich tourism experiences through their own hobbies, cultural activities, and community relationships. In this sense, a structure already exists in which both sides mutually generate value.

This study analyzes a community-participatory Christmas hymn project conducted at Hotel Sunvalley Nasu as a practical case study. In this project, local residents voluntarily organized and performed choir activities within the hotel facility, while tourists themselves were also invited to participate in singing.

In particular, the project incorporated the following elements:

- The Church of the Sun, formerly used as a wedding chapel
- A full church environment including a pipe organ
- Choir participation centered around elderly local residents
- A participatory structure in which tourists also sang together
- Explanations of the historical background of Christmas hymns
- Lyric booklets accompanied by messages of gratitude from the organizer

Through these elements, the project created not merely a passive viewing event, but a tourism experience centered on “sharing time together.”

The Church of the Sun itself had originally been built as a wedding venue, complete with elaborate architecture, interior design, and a grand pipe organ. However, due to declining birthrates and decreasing demand for weddings, the space had gradually become less utilized. For the hotel as well, reactivating this space as a new venue for regional culture had become an important issue.

Furthermore, by briefly explaining the historical background of Christmas hymns and the origins of Santa Claus during the event, tourists were able not only to listen to music, but also to intuitively understand why these songs and traditions have continued across generations.

As a result, the following phenomena were observed:

- Cultural participation by local residents
- Shared participation between tourists and residents
- Intergenerational interaction
- Experiential transmission of culture
- Softening of the boundaries between the local community and the tourism facility

These findings suggest that Hotel Sunvalley Nasu functioned not merely as an accommodation facility, but as a cultural intermediary space connecting local culture and visitors.

This study also focuses on the message of gratitude included within the lyric booklet. Through this message, the meaning that “visitors,” “hosts,” and “residents” were singing together and sharing the same moment was clearly designed and communicated.

This functioned not merely as event management, but as a form of social connection through information design.

Ultimately, this study proposes the possibility of a new regional coexistence model that transforms tourism from a consumption-based experience into a co-generated experience.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background

The Nasu region has long developed as one of Japan’s representative highland resort areas. Supported by rich natural environments, hot springs, villas, leisure facilities, and museums, the region has continuously attracted large numbers of visitors.

At the same time, however, the region has also begun facing structural issues common to many rural communities in Japan, including population decline, aging demographics, shrinking local economies, weakening community activities, a decline in privately owned local businesses, and a shortage of successors.

In tourism regions especially, spaces designed for tourists and the everyday lives of local residents often become separated. Tourism facilities receive visitors, while local residents are positioned mainly as service providers. As a result, meaningful interaction between tourists and residents may remain limited.

Large-scale accommodation facilities are important tourism infrastructures that support local economies. However, they are also sometimes discussed as competitors to local businesses.

In reality, however, the visitor-attracting power, spatial resources, and cultural resources possessed by large-scale accommodation facilities can potentially be connected with the hobbies, cultural activities, and human relationships of local residents.

This study focuses on that potential for connection.

### 1.2 Research Problem

Many conventional tourism events are structured around the following elements:

- Invitation of external artists
- One-way appreciation by the audience
- Commercial staging
- Consumption-oriented experience

Within these structures, tourists tend to remain “viewers,” while local residents tend to be fixed in the role of “providers.”

As a result, opportunities for the following remain limited:

- Community participation
- Cultural inheritance
- Intergenerational interaction

At the same time, many regional revitalization projects tend to focus on creating something new. However, local communities already contain many resources, including:

- Music
- People
- Space
- Culture
- Elderly residents’ lived experience
- Tourism facilities

This study examines whether a new structure of regional coexistence can be formed by reconnecting these existing elements.

### 1.3 Research Purpose

This study aims to reconstruct the relationship among Hotel Sunvalley Nasu, tourists, and local residents as a “mutual co-creation structure” rather than a structure based on consumption, labor, or competition.

Using the community-participatory Christmas hymn project held at Hotel Sunvalley Nasu as a case study, this research analyzes how the following elements influenced the tourism experience:

- Voluntary cultural activities by local residents
- Visitor-participatory singing
- Participatory design through lyric booklets
- Messages of gratitude from the organizer
- Cultural understanding through explanations of hymn backgrounds

The study further examines this project not merely as a successful event example, but as a social structure in which multiple elements were connected, including:

- Spatial reactivation
- Cultural inheritance
- Elderly participation
- Tourism experience design
- Information design

## 2. Background / Literature Review

### 2.1 Large-Scale Accommodation Facilities in Tourism Regions

Large-scale accommodation facilities function as core infrastructures within tourism regions.

Resort hotels in particular comprehensively support the following:

- Accommodation
- Dining
- Hot springs
- Events
- Tourist attraction

In doing so, they sustain the flow of visitors throughout the region.

However, the spatial resources, gathering power, and cultural influence possessed by large-scale accommodation facilities can function not only as commercial assets, but also as platforms for mediating regional culture.

This study therefore positions large-scale accommodation facilities not simply as lodging facilities, but as devices for connecting regional culture.

### 2.2 Participatory Tourism Research

In recent years, tourism studies have increasingly emphasized concepts such as:

- Participatory Tourism
- Community-based Tourism
- Experience Design

However, many of these studies focus primarily on the following:

- Consumption of regional experiences
- Activity participation
- Economic outcomes

They have not sufficiently discussed shared experiential structures such as:

- Singing together
- Spending the same time together
- Sharing emotions collectively

This study focuses specifically on the structure in which “spending time together” itself becomes tourism value.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 Research Subject

This study examines a community-participatory Christmas hymn project held at Hotel Sunvalley Nasu.

The project consisted of voluntary choir activities by local residents within the hotel facility, while tourists themselves were also invited to participate in singing. Approximately twenty-five participants, including the organ performer, joined the event, and many of the participants were elderly residents.

In this study, the voices of elderly participants are interpreted not merely as musical techniques, but as expressions formed through long years of lived experience.

Just as musical instruments deepen their tone over time, human voices also accumulate unique resonance through the following:

- Hardship
- Joy
- Community life
- Historical background
- Personal life experience

Accordingly, this study treats the “time lived within the voice” itself as a form of regional cultural resource.

In contemporary society, many elderly individuals feel that they have already completed their social role. At the same time, younger generations often feel that such accumulated experience is precisely what local communities still need.

This study therefore also examines how reconnecting these two perspectives may contribute to the following:

- Reconstruction of elderly social roles
- Transformation of experiential knowledge into regional resources
- New forms of participation within long-life societies

Furthermore, this study reconsiders the definition of tourism itself.

Conventionally, tourism is often discussed as the following:

- Viewing landscapes
- Staying at accommodations
- Enjoying food and services

However, this project focuses on experiences such as:

- Singing together with local residents
- Sharing space with other tourists
- Spending the same time together

The study examines how these experiences create emotional attachment toward a place.

Particular attention was also given to emotional and spatial responses that are difficult to quantify, including:

- Smiles of tourists
- Family atmosphere
- Natural conversations
- A sense of unity within the space

Additionally, lyric booklets, explanations of hymns, and messages of gratitude were analyzed not merely as supplementary materials, but as forms of information design intended to connect people, culture, and space.

Here, information design does not simply refer to the transmission of information. Rather, it refers to the social design of shared meaning, including the following:

- Why people sing
- Why people gather
- Why people spend time together

Through this, information design enables natural human connection.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Community-Led Cultural Formation

In this project, the choir was formed not through dependence on external performers, but through cultural activities initiated by local residents themselves.

Choir performances generally require large numbers of participants and therefore tend to become costly in commercial tourism settings. As a result, hotel events and tourism performances are often limited to the following formats:

- One or two singers
- Small-scale musical arrangements
- Instrument-centered performances

Consequently, opportunities for ordinary tourists to experience live choral singing are relatively rare.

However, in this project, the choir was sustained through the following:

- Hobby-based activities
- Community interaction
- Voluntary participation

Through these foundations, a large-scale choral environment was able to emerge naturally.

More importantly, this choir was not merely a musical performance. It was composed of “the voices of people living within the community itself.”

The voices of elderly participants especially contained depth shaped through long years of lived experience and regional life. This produced warmth and emotional resonance distinct from mere technical musical skill.

This represents a case in which human resources and accumulated experiential knowledge already existing within the community were reconnected as tourism value.

### 4.2 Transformation of Tourist Experience Through Participation

Tourists did not remain passive audience members, but actively participated in singing.

As a result, the following scenes were observed:

- Families smiling together
- Couples singing together
- Children naturally joining the singing
- Tourists smiling toward one another

Particularly among families with children, not only the children but also the parents themselves expressed reactions such as “This is incredibly fun.”

This suggests that the project created not merely an event for viewing, but a structure in which entire families participated in a shared experience together.

Furthermore, through collective singing participation, the boundaries among the following groups temporarily softened:

- Local residents
- Hotel staff
- Tourists

In that moment, they came to share a common identity as “people singing together.”

Most importantly, the act of “having sung together” itself became a core element that remained in visitors’ memories.

### 4.3 Experiential Understanding of Culture

This project did not simply present music performances. It also incorporated brief explanations concerning the following:

- Historical backgrounds of hymns
- Origins of songs
- Cultural history of Santa Claus

Through these explanations, visitors were able to intuitively understand not only the music itself, but also why these cultural traditions have continued across generations.

For example, “O Holy Night” was introduced as a piece that had initially been criticized by churches due to controversies surrounding its background and composer, yet eventually became beloved worldwide because of the beauty of its melody.

Similarly, the origin of Santa Claus was explained through the story of Saint Nicholas secretly placing gold coins into the stockings of impoverished children.

Visitors responded with comments such as “I see,” often accompanied by smiles.

These explanations functioned not merely as knowledge transmission, but as a way of sharing reasons for participating in the culture itself.

Furthermore, through messages of gratitude included within the lyric booklets, the project clearly communicated the idea that the following people were all sharing the same moment together:

- Visitors
- Hosts
- Residents

This functioned not simply as event guidance, but as a form of information design sharing the meaning of why people gather together within the same space.

### 4.4 Emotional Impact on Community Participants

Emotional changes were observed not only among tourists, but also among local participants themselves.

One choir participant was seen in tears after the performance.

This reaction may have emerged not merely from a sense of musical accomplishment, but from the feeling that the following elements had become connected as one shared experience:

- Local residents
- Tourists
- The hotel
- Space
- Music

Additionally, following the event, the hotel expressed a desire for the project to continue annually.

This suggests that the project was recognized not merely as a one-time entertainment event, but as something valuable for the following:

- Regional cultural formation
- Enhancement of tourism value
- Reactivation of underutilized spaces
- Reconnection between the hotel and the local community

Most importantly, this structure was created not through large-scale external entertainment, but through community-led participation.

The value observed in this study therefore began to be recognized not as a “consumed event,” but as a cultural structure capable of growing together with the region itself.

### 4.5 Successful Reactivation of Space

The Church of the Sun was originally designed as a wedding chapel and was equipped with the following:

- A pipe organ
- Church-style architecture
- Symbolic lighting design

However, due to declining marriage rates and changing social conditions, the space had gradually become less utilized than before.

Through this project, the space was once again filled with the following:

- Singing voices
- Human presence
- Culture
- Emotion

As a result, the original meaning and value of the space were reconnected.

This was not merely an example of event utilization. It was a case in which the original significance of the space itself was culturally reactivated through regional participation.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Co-Generation of Tourism Experience

The tourism experience observed in this study was not simply an act of “using a facility,” but rather an act of “sharing time with others.”

In this case, visitors were transformed from passive observers into people collectively creating the atmosphere itself.

In particular, the act of “singing together” involved the sharing of the following:

- Space
- Emotion
- Time

Through this, the tourism experience itself was deepened.

### 5.2 Reconnection of Existing Regional Resources

One of the most important findings of this study is that the project did not rely on creating entirely new resources.

Already existing within the region were the following:

- Church space
- Pipe organ
- Music
- Elderly residents
- Tourists
- Hotel
- Christmas culture

The project’s achievement lay in reconnecting these elements in meaningful ways.

This suggests that regional revitalization may depend less on producing constant novelty and more on reconnecting values already present within the community.

### 5.3 Regional Connection Through Information Design

This study treated lyric booklets, historical explanations, and organizer messages as forms of information design connecting people, culture, and space.

Here, information design does not simply refer to the transmission of information.

Rather, it involves sharing the meanings of the following:

- Why people sing
- Why people gather
- Why people express gratitude

Through this sharing of meaning, natural human connection becomes possible.

Through this information design, the boundaries among tourists, residents, and hotel staff gradually softened and became interconnected.

### 5.4 Reconstruction of Elderly Roles

This study positioned elderly participants not merely as recipients of support, but as central figures sustaining regional culture.

The experiences and emotional expressions carried by elderly residents may also be interpreted as forms of community-based experiential knowledge accumulated through long years of regional life.

Particularly within choral singing, the human voice itself became an expression of lived experience, adding depth and emotional richness to the entire space.

Furthermore, when younger generations expressed that “such experience is needed,” elderly participants themselves began to rediscover a sense of social role and meaning. This suggests possibilities for renewed participation within contemporary aging societies.

## 6. Conclusion

This study analyzed the mutual co-creation structure formed among local residents, tourists, and Hotel Sunvalley Nasu through a community-participatory Christmas hymn project.

The findings suggest that tourism can be reconstructed not merely as an act of consumption, but as an experience of sharing time and collectively shaping space together.

The study also demonstrated that new tourism value emerged through reconnecting elements already existing within the region, including the following:

- Church space
- Pipe organ
- Local residents
- Elderly participants
- Music
- Culture
- Visitors

This suggests that regional revitalization may not necessarily depend on constantly creating new resources, but rather on reconnecting values that already exist within a community.

Furthermore, these connections were supported through forms of information design, including the following:

- Lyric booklets
- Historical explanations of hymns
- Messages of gratitude
- Participation guidance

The structure observed in this study functioned not merely as event management, but as a social system connecting people, culture, space, and emotion.

Most importantly, this study revealed that local residents, hotel staff, and visitors—despite occupying different social positions—shared a sense of “spending the same moment together.”

By singing together, listening together, and sharing the atmosphere of Christmas together, the boundaries among the following gradually softened:

- Visitors
- Hosts
- Residents

Hotel Sunvalley Nasu itself began functioning as a shared regional cultural space.

Within small rural communities in particular, existing forms of information—people, culture, space, and emotion—can often be more carefully organized and recombined, allowing new forms of social connection to emerge.

Such structures of reconnection may become new models for regional communities facing similar challenges, and as these successes accumulate, they may eventually serve as templates for reconstructing community structures within urban society as well.
